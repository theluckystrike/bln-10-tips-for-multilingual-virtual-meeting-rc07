What I Learned Running Multilingual Calls

Originally published at belikenative.com/10-tips-for-multilingual-virtual-meetings

My first multilingual team call was a disaster. Three people spoke over each other, the interpreter dropped off after ten minutes, and half the team couldn't follow the slides. I've run about two hundred of these calls since then, and I've picked up a few things that actually help.

Send language details before the meeting

I used to think a calendar invite was enough. It isn't. Now I send a short note two or three days ahead that lists the languages we'll cover, whether we're using AI captions or a human interpreter, and a link to any translated documents. This alone cut our post-meeting "what did they mean?" messages by roughly a third.

The format doesn't need to be fancy. A few bullet points in the invite body work fine. I include which translation method we're using, the languages available, and instructions for turning on captions in whatever platform we're on.

Pick software that does live translation well

Not every platform handles multiple languages the same way. I tested several and ended up sticking with one that offered real-time captions with auto-detection, translation memory for consistent terminology, and support across desktop and mobile. Testing language pairs before the actual call saved me from some embarrassing moments early on.

I also started using BeLikeNative alongside my meeting platform. It handles quick translations in the chat window and helps me prep multilingual materials before the call starts. That combination of a solid platform plus a browser-based translation tool covered most of my needs.

Translate your documents ahead of time

Sharing a translated agenda might sound obvious, but I skipped it for months. Turns out, when people can read the agenda in their own language before the call, the meeting moves faster. I now send translated agendas two days out and presentation decks at least a day before.

For quick translations on the fly, BeLikeNative has been my go-to. It works with over 80 languages and keeps terminology consistent, which matters when you're dealing with technical content. I paste in the text, pick the target language, and clean up anything that sounds off.

Brief your interpreters properly

I learned this the hard way. An interpreter who gets the agenda, speaker list, and a glossary of industry terms 48 to 72 hours in advance will outperform one who's reading the slides for the first time during the call. Most interpretation errors I've seen came from interpreters not having enough prep time.

Give them speaker profiles too. Knowing that one presenter talks fast and another uses a lot of acronyms helps the interpreter plan ahead. A short pre-call check-in, even ten minutes, makes a noticeable difference.

Slow down and simplify

This one is hard to remember in the moment. I tend to talk fast, and I used to rely on idioms without thinking. Both are problems in multilingual settings. Now I aim for short sentences, pause between ideas, and avoid slang entirely. When I catch myself using a phrase that won't translate well, I rephrase on the spot.

Checking in with participants helps too. A simple "does that make sense so far?" gives people a chance to ask questions without feeling like they're interrupting.

Use visuals and captions together

Slides with clear diagrams do more than a wall of text ever will. I add multilingual captions when possible and keep slide text minimal. The combination of something visual and something readable in their language keeps more people engaged through the whole call.

Make space for quieter voices

In a multilingual call, the people who need the most time to formulate their thoughts are often the ones who stay silent. I started building in explicit pauses and sometimes asking specific people for their input. Written chat channels running alongside the main call give people another way to contribute without the pressure of speaking up.

Different cultures approach meetings differently too. Some participants prefer indirect communication and won't interrupt a senior colleague. Being aware of that changed how I run the call. Going around the virtual room for input, rather than waiting for volunteers, helped a lot.

Follow up in every language

After the meeting, I send a summary with action items translated into each participant's preferred language. This takes extra time, but it prevents the confusion that comes from people misunderstanding their assignments. I include a recording with captions when I can, so anyone who missed the call or wants to review can catch up on their own schedule.

The whole process got easier once I stopped treating multilingual support as an afterthought. As my team keeps growing across time zones, BeLikeNative stays in my workflow for prepping materials and translating follow-up notes.
